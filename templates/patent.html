Patent Law in a Nutshell

by Isaak Cuenco-Reyes

Origin of the “Patent” right: Article 1 Section 8 Clause 8 – congress shall have the power to control progress in technology and useful arts.

If you are a start up company, why would you want a patent?

  • Long-lasting product protection (14 years design; 20 years utility)
  • Can exclude new competition
  • Can seek civil and criminal damages for patent infringement, thereby discouraging others from unlawful use. (This creates a barrier to entry for other firms allowing the monopoly to earn abnormal profits.)
  • Signals value to investors of innovation / creativity / protective value (NOTE: “Patent Pending” means nothing to investors.)
  • Protection from industry monopolies (may also allow monopoly to earn rents from licensing the patent to other firms.)

Patent Requirements:

  • Novelty
  • Non-obviousness
  • Usefulness
  • Subject matter eligible (cannot be naturally occurring / laws of nature / abstract ideas)
  • Submitted description: Written specific description that enables someone to make the invention (*quid pro quo)
  • Written Claim / Diagram (Design Patents = you own the Diagram, Utility Patent = you own Written Claim). One sentence definition / description of the patent / subject matter that is sufficiently definite.

Utility vs. Design Patents

A “utility patent” protects the way an article is used and works (35 U.S.C. 101), while a “design patent” protects the way an article looks (35 U.S.C. 171). The ornamental appearance for an article includes its shape/configuration or surface ornamentation applied to the article, or both.

Utility Patents

Benefits

  • Protects the functional aspects of an invention.
  • Can provide broad patent protection making it difficult for a competing product to avoid patent infringement.
  • Capable of protecting many different variations of a product with a single utility patent

Limitations

  • More expensive than a design patent.
  • Takes longer to receive patent protection (normally 2-3 years).
  • Does not protect the ornamental features of an invention

Design Patents

Benefits

  • Historically cheaper and easier to obtain than utility patent.
  • Usually faster patent protection at the U.S. Patent Office (normally between 1-2 years). If the main feature of the new product is the appearance (i.e. ornamental design), then a design patent will protect this main feature.

Limitations

  • Design patents do not protect the functional features of an invention (most inventions have functional features).
  • Design patents can be relatively easy for competing products to subvert / design around by simply changing an element of ornamentation.
  • Difficult to protect different variations of the same product.

The Economics of Intellectual Property Patents

The basic rationalization of intellectual property rights is the tradeoff between static and dynamic efficiency. IP rights provide, ex ante, exclusivity in the commercial exploitation of innovations. In turn, such innovations – whether new products, processes, or creative works – provide the impetus of economic growth.
