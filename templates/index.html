Copyright Law

Copyright is a legal right over the use and distribution of an original work granted to its creator, usually for a limited time. These exclusive rights are not absolute and are governed by Title 17 of the US Code (USC 17), which outlines fundamentals of copyright law and its exceptions, including fair use. Copyrightable works must be original expressions of an idea that are not functional products; an idea itself also cannot be copyrighted.

Trademark Law

Trademarks are symbols or devices that are legally registered or established by use as representative marks of a company or product. Chapter 22 of US Code Title 15 outlines trademark law, defining what is meant by "symbols or devices" and establishing what sorts of things are eligible for trademarks. Examples of trademarkable symbols include colors, sounds, smells, one or more words, and stylized images.

Patent Law

Patents are exclusive rights over development and sale of a novel functional product, device, or process, granted to the inventor for ~20 years. Patent applications must consist of drawings, written description of the product, and claims detailing precisely what is being patented. An inventor may apply for a utility patent or a design patent, although the former is more common since design patents often overlap with copyrights.
